# Formula Reverse Engineering Skill

This skill outlines a rigorous, 7-step iterative methodology for reverse-engineering underlying "elegant" mathematical formulas from black-box datasets (e.g., extracting scoring algorithms or physical models from raw input/output data). 

When tasked with finding an underlying formula, DO NOT simply throw the data into a generic polynomial or neural network fit. Instead, strictly follow this iterative workflow.

## 1. Exploratory Fitting
- Your goal in the early stages is breadth, not extreme precision.
- Test multiple input features and their combinations against the target value.
- Attempt basic linear fits, logarithmic transformations, and power law models to establish a baseline understanding of the solution space.

## 2. Isolate & Control Components
- As the experiment progresses, the exact mathematical relationship of certain variables will become clear.
- Once you mathematically verify that a specific component (e.g., `Sharpe` being perfectly linear with power 1.0) is absolutely correct, **lock its parameters immediately**.
- Extract this known component as a constant term to reduce the dimensionality of the remaining problem. You must strip away known truths to focus on the remaining unknown dimensions with higher precision.

## 3. Outlier Analysis
- After stripping away a known layer, shift your focus entirely to the data points with the largest residuals (Outliers).
- Isolate the metadata/labels of these outliers.
- The fundamental purpose of analyzing outliers is to **locate and verify the underlying cause of the anomaly**, thereby exposing the flaws or missing variables in your current working formula.

## 4. Slicing & Segmentation
- Observe the data distribution and search for boundaries or kinks. If different ranges of data exhibit drastically different slopes or behaviors, split the data and fit them separately. DO NOT force a single complex continuous equation to fit everything initially.
- Slicing serves two distinct purposes in the wild:
  1. **Reconstructing True Thresholds:** The underlying business logic might have hard thresholds (e.g., floors, ceilings, asymmetric positive/negative scoring). Slicing helps recreate these exact logical branches.
  2. **Tactical Dimension Reduction:** When the exact function of certain variables is unknown, slice the data into narrow bands (or find natural control variables, like a dimension being exactly `0`). This eliminates the noise from unknown dimensions, providing a pure, highly accurate environment to solve the known dimensions.

## 5. Polynomial Probing & Shape Guessing
- When you successfully isolate an unknown single-variable function, do not guess complex formulas blindly.
- The safest approach is to first use a high-order polynomial to perform a purely mathematical fit and plot the discrete points into a continuous curve.
- By observing the geometric shape of this polynomial curve (e.g., convex/concave, presence of asymptotes, zero-intercepts), cross-reference it with standard function libraries to guess a simpler, more physically meaningful analytical expression (such as logarithmic, exponential, arcsinh, or trigonometric functions).

## 6. Fusion
- Slicing is a means, not an end. 
- As model precision improves, if you discover that different data slices are actually governed by the same higher-dimensional mathematical law (e.g., two disjointed segmented lines are actually just different parts of the same continuous logarithmic or exponential curve), boldly merge the segments back into a unified equation. Fusion drastically improves the model's generalizability.

## 7. Sub-optimization & Physical Elegance
- Initial high-precision models generated by machines are often filled with "ugly", overfitted decimal noise (e.g., `206.04 * X^2.01`). You must perform sub-optimization aimed at "Physical Elegance":
  - **Optimize Variable Combinations (Algebraic Simplification):** Simplify complex fractional or polynomial structures into concise, standard business/physical ratios (e.g., reducing `Returns / (Drawdown + k * Returns)` directly into the `Calmar Ratio`). If the simplified model still explains the variance, you have found the core feature.
  - **Optimize Coefficients (Human Bias Convergence):** Engineered systems are designed by humans. Humans naturally prefer simple integers, clean fractions, and industry-standard constants (e.g., 250 trading days). Force the machine's "ugly decimals" to snap to meaningful integers or clean fractions. If the residual error does not significantly increase (or even drops to zero due to eliminating error cancellation), it proves you have uncovered the true underlying design soul of the formula.
